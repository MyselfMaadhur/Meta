Sources of Ancient Indian History

5 Major categories of evidence

▼

🔬 Archaeological Evidence (Artefacts)

  • Physical materials used by ancient people found at sites
  • Crucial for understanding the Pre-historic Period

📜 Inscriptions (Epigraphy)

  • Engravings on stone or metal objects
  • Study of inscriptions = Epigraphy
  • Types: Rock edicts (Major & Minor), Pillar edicts

🪙 Coins (Numismatics)

  • Study of coins = Numismatics
  • Reveals economic conditions, trade, and rulers

📚 Literature

  • Indian Literature: Vedas, Puranas, Epics, Sangam texts
  • Foreign Literature: Accounts by Greek, Chinese travelers

🌍 Accounts of Foreigners

  • Greek, Chinese, and other foreign travelers' records
  • Provide external perspective on Indian conditions

1. Introduction & Naming

Discovery, names, and basic facts

▼

  • First site excavated — Harappa by Dayaram Sahni in 1921

3. Town Planning & Structures

City layout, buildings, and remarkable features

▼

🏗️ City Division

  • City divided into two parts:
  • Upper Citadel — city on raised platform
  • Lower Citadel — lower town

📐 Grid System

  • Grid system followed in arrangement of houses and roads

🧱 Burnt Bricks

  • Usage of burnt bricks — a remarkable feature of Harappan

🏊 Great Bath

  • Great Bath served as ritual bathing
  • Found in upper citadel of Mohenjo-Daro

🌾 Granaries

  • A large granary in Mohenjo-Daro
  • Six granaries in Harappa

🏠 Barracks

  • Two-roomed barracks in Harappa
  • Possibly accommodated labourers

🚰 Drainage System

  • Drainage system was another remarkable feature of the civilization

4. Agriculture & Trade

Crops, irrigation, commerce, and weights

▼

🌱 Fertility

  • The Indus region was fertile in ancient times
  • Otherwise it would not have supported towns and villages

🌾 Crops Grown

  • Wheat
  • Barley
  • Peas
  • Sesame
  • Mustard
  • In Lothal, people started producing rice

👕 Cotton

  • Indus people were the first to produce cotton

💧 Irrigation

  • Structures used to store water — Gabarbands or Nalas
  • But channel or canal irrigation seems to have been absent

🚢 Trade

  • Indus valley people had both short and long-distance trade
  • Also foreign trade
  • Used boats and bullock-carts for transportation

💰 Currency & Exchange

  • No use of metallic currency
  • Practised barter system of exchange

⚖️ Weights & Measures

  • Developed uniform system of weights and measures

🔖 Seals & Sealings

  • Seals and sealings were used in trade
  • As marks of ownership meant to guarantee the quality of the product

7. Religion & Religious Practices

Deities, worship, burials, and beliefs

▼

🏛️ Collective Worship

  • Presence of some structures with ritual significance indicate existence of collective worship

🏊 Great Bath

  • Great Bath, Mohenjo-Daro had a great ritual significance

🌱 Fertility Cult & Mother Goddess

  • Prevalence of cults of fertility and mother goddess worship

🌳 Tree Spirits

  • They worshipped tree spirits
  • Pipal tree was worshipped

🕉️ Proto-Shiva (Pashupati)

  • On the seals, a famous deity identified as proto-Shiva was found
  • The deity is surrounded by animals like goats, elephant, tiger, and antelope

🔱 Phallic Worship

  • There is sufficient evidence for the prevalence of phallic worship
  • Numerous stone symbols of female sex organs (yoni worship)
  • Besides those of phallus, have been discovered

🔥 Fire Worship

  • The worship of fire is proved by the discovery of fire altars
  • At Lothal, Kalibangan, and Harappa

👻 Beliefs

  • They believed in ghosts and evil forces
  • Used amulets as protection against them

⚰️ Disposal of the Dead

  • Disposal of the dead has been an important religious activity
  • Dead bodies were generally placed in the north-south direction
  • Nearby the dead body the ornaments and other objects were also buried
Key Feature

Fertility cult was the main feature of Harappan religion

5. Society: Early vs Later Vedic Period

Social structure, women, and varna system

▼

🏛️ Early Vedic Society

  • Kinship was the basis of social structure
  • People gave primary loyalty to the tribe (Jana)
  • Another important term for tribe: Vis
  • Term for family (kula) mentioned rarely
  • Family in early Vedic phase indicated by term griha

👩 Women in Early Vedic Period

  • Institution of marriage was established
  • Practice of levirate and widow remarriage in the Rig Veda
  • Status of women was equal to men
  • They received Upanayana and education
  • Studied Vedas; some rose to rank of seers composing Vedic hymns
  • Monogamy established, though polygamy and polyandry were also known

🎨 Varna System — Early Vedic

  • Varna was the term used for color
  • Aryans were fair; indigenous inhabitants were dark
  • The dasas and Dasyus, conquered by Aryans, were treated as slaves and sudras
  • Tribal society divided into 3 groups: warriors (Kshatriya), priests (Brahmins), Vaishyas
  • The fourth division called Shudras appeared towards the end of the Rig Veda period
  • Divisions based on occupations had started but not very sharp

🏛️ Later Vedic Society

  • Society came to be divided into 4 varnas: Brahmanas, Rajanyas/Kshatriyas, Vaisyas, Shudras
  • Each Varna was assigned with its duty
  • All 3 higher varnas shared one common feature: known as Dvijas (twice born)
  • Entitled to upanayana or investiture with the sacred thread
  • The fourth Varna was deprived of the sacred thread ceremony
  • With it began the imposition of disabilities on the shudras

🔗 Gotra System — Later Vedic

  • Institution of gotra appeared in later Vedic times
  • Literally means the cow-pen or place where cattle belonging to the whole clan are kept
  • People began to practice gotra exogamy
  • No marriage could take place between persons belonging to the same gotra or having the same ancestor

🧘 Ashrama System — Later Vedic

  • Ashramas or four stages of life were not well established in early Vedic times
  • In post-Vedic texts, four ashramas:
  • Brahmachari — student
  • Grihastha — householder
  • Vanaprastha — partial retirement
  • Sanyasa — complete retirement from the world
  • Only three mentioned in later Vedic texts
  • The 4th Ashrama only mentioned in Jabala Upanishad; not well-established in Later Vedic times

  • Ashoka's Dhamma was different from Buddhism

  • Pay respect to Brahmanas and Buddhist monks
  • Show mercy to slaves and servants

State participation, taxation, and monopoly

👷 Shudras in Agriculture

  • Shudras were involved in agriculture for the first time

🌾 Crown Land

  • Crown land was called Sita

💧 Irrigation

  • There were provisions for irrigation by the state

  • The normal taxation rate was one sixth of the produce

  • The weight and measures were regulated by the states

  • They were collected at gate

  • Mining
  • Forest
  • Salt
  • Sale of liquor
  • Manufacture of arms

🆘 Famine Relief

  • Sohgaura (Gorakhpur district, U.P.) copper plate inscription & Mahasthana (Bogara district, Bangladesh) inscription
  • Deal with the relief measures to be adopted during a famine

11. Causes for Decline

Factors leading to Mauryan decline

▼
  • Highly centralized administration
  • Pacific policy of Ashoka
  • Financial crisis — Bureaucracy expanded but the income remained constant
  • Neglect of North-West frontier

12. Post Mauryan Period

Regional kingdoms and important dynasties

▼

🗺️ Regional Kingdoms

  • After the decline of Mauryas, the regional kingdoms were formed
  • Unlike Mauryas, none of these regional kingdoms could extend their political authority over the large area equivalent to that of Mauryan Empire
  • Thus, with the downfall of Mauryas their great empire witnessed rise of multiple regional kingdoms

🧭 Native Rulers

  • In eastern India, central India and the Deccan, this period saw number of native rulers such as Shungas, the Kanvas and the Satavahanas

🗺️ North-Western Rulers

  • In north-western India, this period witnessed a number of ruling dynasties from central Asia

2. Indo-Greeks (Bactrians)

Political, socio-economic, and cultural contributions

▼

⚔️ Political & Administration

  • Second century BC
  • First to cross Hindukush
  • Pushed towards India by the advancing Scythians tribes
  • Ruled large part of north-western India
  • Famous Indo-Greek King was Menander (165–145 BC)
  • He had his capital at Sakala (Punjab)

💰 Socio-Economic

  • First to issue gold coins in India
  • And can be definitely attributed to a king

🎨 Art and Culture

  • Introduced Hellenistic art features in the region, giving rise to Gandhara art of Buddhism
Gandhara School of Art
  • Greek-Roman art with Indigenous elements
  • Grey sandstone is used
  • Images of this art are known for:
  • Anatomical accuracy
  • Spatial depth
  • Foreshortening
  • Roman influences — outer robe like Kaaya, human form representation, etc.
  • Greek influences — facial images, man-god form etc.

📜 Religion & Literature

  • King Menander, earlier known as Milinda, was converted to Buddhism
  • The questions of Milinda to Nagasena, Buddhist monk, was recorded in form of book known as Milinda Panho

🔬 Technology

  • Use of curtain, known as yavani, got from Greeks
  • Greek term horoscope was derived from the term horasastra

🗣️ Language

  • First ever long inscription was issued in Sanskrit
  • Earlier inscriptions were issued in Prakrit

💧 Irrigation

  • They emphasized on proper irrigation to the region
  • Which was manifested through the repair of Sudarshan lake, by Rudradaman
⚔️

3. The Sakas

Branches, Vikramaditya, and Rudradaman

▼

🏛️ Five Branches

  • They were five branches of the Sakas with their capitals at different regions
  • One branch is located in Afghanistan
  • Other branches:
  • 1. Punjab — Taxila
  • 2. Mathura — Western India
  • 4. Upper Deccan

⚔️ Powerful Branch

  • Out of all the branches, the branch that ruled western India was powerful and existed for four centuries

👑 Vikramaditya

  • In 58 BC, King of Ujjain defeated Sakas
  • And he took title 'Vikramaditya'
  • An era started with 58 BC by Vikramaditya

👑 Most Famous Saka Ruler

  • The most famous Saka ruler was Rudradaman I (130–150 AD)

🤝 Integration

  • Like the Sakas they became integral part of Indian polity and society

✝️ Christianity

  • In the reign of Gondophernes, St. Thomas is said to have come to India for the propagation of Christianity

5. The Kushans (Yuechis or Tocharians)

Empire, administration, religion, and culture

▼

📅 Time Period

  • 50 AD to 230 AD

🗺️ Origin

  • They are the nomadic tribes of north-central Asia

🌍 Empire Extent

  • Their empire extended from the Oxus to the Ganga
  • From Khorasan in Central Asia to Varanasi in Uttar Pradesh

👑 Two Successive Dynasties

  • 1. Kadphises
  • 2. Kanishka

💰 Kadphises

  • Kadphises issued Gold coins in India
  • Kushans extended their power over upper India and the lower Indus

🏛️ Capitals

  • Peshawar and Mathura

👑 Kanishka

  • Kanishka is the most popular Kushan King
  • He started an era called Saka era, 78 AD

⚖️ Administration

  • Strengthened the idea of divine origin of Kingship
  • Introduced Satrap system
  • Military governorship

🙏 Religion

  • They got assimilated into Indian tradition
  • The Kushan rulers worshipped both Shiva and the Buddha
  • Several Kushan rulers worshipped Vishnu
  • Kanishka convened fourth Buddhist council
  • Became a great patron of Mahayana Buddhism

🎨 Mathura School of Art

  • It is an indigenous art developed in Mathura, Sarnath and Kausambi
  • Material used was spotted red stone
  • It covers Buddhism, Jainism and Brahmanism
  • Jina image and Buddha's image are masterpieces of the art
  • It also had secular element

👗 Dress & Technology

  • Introduced turban, tunic, trousers and heavy long coat

⚔️ Military Technology

  • Introduced better cavalry and the use of the riding horse
  • Made common the use of reins and saddles
  • Used toe-stirrup made up of rope to facilitate their movement
  • Introduced cap, helmet and boots

📚 Literature

  • Avaghosha wrote Buddhacharita and Saundarananda
  • Avadanas — compositions of Mahayana Buddhism

🏗️ Architecture

  • Use of burnt bricks for flooring and tiles for both roofing and flooring
  • Construction of brick-wells
  • Red ware — plain and polished
  • Sprinklers and spouted channels
